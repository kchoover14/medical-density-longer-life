Access to Health Care Workers Increases Life Expectancy

Expert Foreign Policy Decisions Using Data

time series | modelling | health care access | oral health | mortality rates
Author

Published

November 19, 2023

Executive Summary

The density of health care workers positively impacts life expectancy outcomes by up to a year. In the 43 countries with density of health workers under 1 per 10,000, the life expectancy was the lowest. The greatest impact on life expectancy in countries with a life expectancy over 60 is from density of dentists. Oral health is often neglected health due to lack of access to a provider (the highest density globally is 15 dentists/10,000 people and the extra expense of visiting a dentist. Yet, poor dental hygiene has deadly outcomes. The recommendation is to use assistance funds to increase density of health workers in countries with mortality rates below 60 and increase density of dentists globally in countries with sufficient medics and nurses/mid-wives.

The Problem

The UN is interested in using part of its assistance budget to increase global life expectancy. The global community recently suffered a dramatic decrease in life expectancy following the COVID-19 pandemic, which was a natural experiment in determining what factors make a place resilient to disaster. A key finding is the importance of a sufficient number of trained health care workers. But even before the pandemic, life expectancy was not consistently stable or consistent across regions. In the graphs below, for example, the Americas experienced declines mid-decade in the 10s. Even wealthy regions, such as the EU and North America, follow this trend, which suggests internal inequities or inadequacies in personnel or funding.

The WHO has projected a global shortfall of 10 million health workers by 2030–low- and lower-middle income countries will suffer the greatest deficits (Health Workforce). This gap is partly attributable to under-investment in education and training of health workers, and also increasing international migration of health workers from these countries to wealthier ones. A great challenge is getting health care workers to rural and remote areas. A final challenge is the lack of universal access to health care workers, which could be offset by public sector investment in care via increased budgets. Ironically, some countries include health workers in their unemployment counts while having major unmet health needs. The WHO High-Level Commission on Health Employment and Economic Growth has identified six pathways to inclusive economic growth through investments in the health and social workforce. In a similar but separate study on health care worker shortfalls, WHO African and Eastern Mediterranean regions will experience the greatest shortfalls and 47 countries on the WHO Support and Safeguards List The global health workforce stock and distribution in 2020 and 2030: a threat to equity and ‘universal’ health coverage?. So, while the industry is rebounding post-pandemic, shortfalls are likely coming and will increase global health inequities.

To determine if investment in health care workers (education, training, support) is a good use of the assistance budget, data from prior to the academic and spanning a 15 year period (2000-2015) were used to study the impact of density of health care workers on life expectancy. Density of dentists, medics, and nurses/mid-wives per 10,000 were variables entered into a linear regression model with life expectancy as the outcome variable. The density of health care workers has a positive impact on life expectancy (adding between 2 months to 1 year of life). The largest effect size was for density of dentist at one year per additional dentist for 10,000 people. Investing assistance funds to countries with low density of health care workers, particularly boosting dentist density, will achieve the goal to increase global life expectancy. A caveat is the global downturn in health care workers following the pandemic due to burn-out. In 2025, the impact of medics and nurses/mid-wives needs to be revisited as those effect sizes might increase.

Results of Data Analysis

Two countries in Asia (Afghanistan, Cambodia), two countries in Oceania (Timor-Leste, Papua New Guinea), and 39 countries in Africa (see Supplemental Materials: Scatterplots for full list). These countries would be primary targets for aid. A linear regression model using three variables for health care density as independent variables, indicated that the greatest impact on life expectancy is from increasing the number of dentists–for every 1 additional dentist per 10,000 people, life expectancy increases by almost one full year (11.64 months). The effect size is large at .39. THe impact on increasing medics is neglible, but for each additional nurse/mid-wife per 10,000 people, 18 days are added to life expectancy The effect size is small at 0.06. There are a maximum of 15 dentists per 10,000 people compared to 75 for medics and 202 nurses/mid-wives.

While this may seem surprising, poor oral health may result in serious health outcomes that eventually result in death. For example, a single carie (bacterial infection) may result in tooth loss, gum inflammation, and spread to other teeth. Over time an abscess can result and lead to septicemia, a life-threatening condition even with excellent medical facilities and care. The WHO Global Oral Health Status Report](https://www.who.int/team/noncommunicable-diseases/global-status-report-on-oral-health-2022/) finds that oral diseases are the most widespread noncommunicable diseases and that they affect 45% (3.5 billion) people worldwide. If almost half the world’s population suffers from a treatable disease, an intervention is needed such as the one proposed in this report–increased density of dentists.

Dental problems have been killing people since the rise of agriculture, and even in [hunter-gatherers eating starchier foods regularly] (https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1318176111). Because dental care may not always be included in health care plans/insurance, many in the US do not have regular dental check-ups. [Dental caries is the most common and pervasive chronic disease in the world. It afflicts more than nine in 10 Americans and billions of people across the globe.] (https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/why-we-have-so-many-problems-with-our-teeth/)

The Solution

This brief reports that density of health care workers increases life expectancy, but that greater density of dentists has an exponentially greater postive impact on life expectancy (just under 12 additional months) compared to 18 additional days from mid-wives and noticable impact from increasing medics. There are far fewer dentists (15) and far greater numbers of nurses (202) per 10,000 people, with medics in the middle (75). Assistance funds should be used to increase health care workers in the 43 countries listed in Supplemental Materials as having almost no health care workers and the lowest life expectancy in the world. Further, increasing the number of dentists should be prioritized in areas where dental care is under-served compared to density of other medical workers.

What we don’t know is how life expectancy will recover in the next ten years, post-pandemic. Every region took a downturn in life expectancy but many factors may change the trajectories reported at the beginning that captured data from 2000-2015, such as the inequities that exist at a global scale, the weaknesses in supply chain revealed by the pandemic, the anticipated shortfall in health care workers, and the war in Ukraine and Israeli actions against Hama in Gaza. The model presented here is using information from a different era and will need to be re-examined for robusticity and models comparing pre- and post-pandemic will be most useful in determining if new intervention strategies are needed.

Another unknown is how to increase the health care workforce (given the challenges presented above with the predicted shortfalls) and retain those in it. Under normal circumstances, burnout in medical professionals affects between one-third to half the critical care physicians on the job. As reported in The Lancet, however, the pandemic took a heavy toll on all health care workers who were suffering from pandemic fatigue, chronic stress, and PTSD. They were fearful of infection, fearful of infecting loved ones, fearful of losing patients, and overwhelmed by patient suffering, losses, and sheer numbers. Their ability to off aid was hampered by low supplies and lack of political intervention to support health care workers and promote vaccination. Depression and anxiety persist in front-line workers. At local scale, women were subject to greater stresses as the pandemic naturally increased gender disparities in the home and at work. At a global scale, lower-to-middle-income countries (LMIC) were hard hit with shortages of personal protection equipment to protect them from infection, an increased workload, and less training and experience in adequately control infection. Thus, inequities in the system and globally are remaining challenges.

The propsed roadmap to invest strategically in narrowing gaps in density of health care workers, with a special focus on dentists, will mitigate global social health inequities. A balanced strategy of recruitment might ease some of the gendered burden in the health care system and greater political intervention (especially financial support) will help bolster a system under extreme stress. Distribution of Independent Variable

Histograms visualize data distributions. For linear models, the independent term (here, life expectancy) is assumed to be normally tested.

Life expectancy data are a classic example of non-normally distributed data. They are negatively skewed. The histograms confirms the life expectancy data in this sample are not Gaussian. The descdist function in the fitdistrplus package how the observed distribution lies outside normal. The fitdist function was indicates that the data are well-fitted to a Weibull distribution. The common transformation for non-normal data is a log transformation, but that tends to increase negative skew and cannot be used with life expectancy data (see example showing increased negative skew from both transformations). Another approach might be to take the square root, but that doesn’t solve the problem. Ultimately, survival analysis is the most appropriate approach for life expectancy data, but that is outside the remit of the policy brief. Another approach might be time series data regression, but that is more complex and outside the remit of the brief.

This analysis is not a scientific study that requires robust confidence intervals to assess the strength of a p-value and high accuracy of the predictor. Rather, the goal is to understand factors that might increase life expectancy. The models will be built with a non-transformed variable. Afterwords, residuals will be plotted and existing departures from normality will be further explored in scatterplots, which will help understand where the model becomes less reliable.

Data Analysis

There were three indicators that assessed the density of health care: dentists, medics, and nurses and mid-wives but limiting to countries with values at 1 or greater. The residuals are normally distributed suggesting a reliable model. The effect size of each indicator was estimated using the eta_squared function in the effectsize package. The distribution and plots of residuals are slightly patterned, likely due to the negative skew of the data. Since these data are not for scientific precision but for informing policy, a data science approach can be taken recognizing that the effect is not consistent across the data. Adding the region into the linear model improves the distribution away from skew but some patterning still exists in the residuals. Scaling at a regional level might be more valuable.
